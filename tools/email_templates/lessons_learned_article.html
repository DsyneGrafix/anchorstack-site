This Lesson Took Me Years — You Can Learn It in Minutes

I spent over a decade learning this lesson the hard way. It cost me relationships, opportunities, and countless sleepless nights. But you don't have to repeat my mistakes. Here's what I wish someone had told me from the beginning.

The Lesson That Changed Everything

After years of struggling with inconsistent results, broken promises to myself, and the constant feeling that I was spinning my wheels, I discovered something that transformed my entire approach to success:

Your identity determines your actions, and your actions determine your results.

It sounds simple, but this realization fundamentally changed how I approached every goal, every challenge, and every opportunity. Let me show you what I mean.

How I Got It Wrong for Years

For the longest time, I focused entirely on outcomes. I wanted to have more money, so I tried to do what rich people do. I wanted to have a successful business, so I tried to do what successful entrepreneurs do.

This approach led to a cycle of temporary motivation followed by inevitable burnout:

  • Week 1: I'd start a new routine with incredible enthusiasm
  • Week 2-3: I'd push through even when motivation waned
  • Week 4: I'd hit a rough patch and start making excuses
  • Week 5+: I'd quit and feel like a failure

I thought I lacked discipline or willpower. The truth was, I was fighting against my own identity.

My Biggest Mistake: I was trying to act like someone I wasn't, instead of becoming the person who would naturally take those actions.

The Identity-First Approach

Everything changed when I flipped the script. Instead of asking "What do I need to do?" I started asking "Who do I need to become?"

Instead of trying to force myself to wake up at 5 AM, I worked on becoming the type of person who values their morning routine. Instead of forcing myself to exercise, I worked on becoming someone who sees fitness as non-negotiable.

The difference was profound. When your actions align with your identity, they don't feel like work—they feel like who you are.

The Three Levels of Change

Most people try to change at the wrong level. There are three levels where change can occur:

  • Outcome Level: What you want to have or achieve
  • Process Level: What you do or the systems you follow
  • Identity Level: What you believe about yourself

The most effective way to change is to start at the identity level and work your way out, not the other way around.

Key Insight: When you change your identity, your behaviors naturally follow. When you only change your behaviors, your old identity will eventually pull you back.

How to Apply This Starting Today

Here's the practical framework I wish someone had given me years ago:

Step 1: Define Your Desired Identity

Instead of saying "I want to lose 20 pounds," say "I am someone who takes care of their body." Instead of "I want to build a business," say "I am an entrepreneur who creates value for others."

Step 2: Vote for Your New Identity

Every action you take is a vote for the type of person you believe yourself to be. You don't need to be perfect—you just need to win the majority of your votes.

If you're becoming a writer, every day you write is a vote for "I am a writer." If you're becoming healthier, every healthy meal is a vote for "I am someone who takes care of their body."

Step 3: Start Ridiculously Small

The goal isn't to transform overnight. It's to prove to yourself that you are the type of person you want to become. Start with actions so small they feel almost silly:

  • Writer: Write one sentence per day
  • Athlete: Do one push-up per day
  • Reader: Read one page per day
  • Entrepreneur: Reach out to one potential customer per day

The Compound Effect of Identity

Here's what I discovered: Small actions compound into strong identities, and strong identities lead to remarkable results.

When I started writing one paragraph per day, I wasn't trying to become a bestselling author. I was just trying to become someone who writes. But after months of daily writing, I naturally started taking on bigger writing projects because that's what writers do.

The Magic: When your identity shifts, opportunities that seemed impossible suddenly become obvious next steps.

Why This Works When Everything Else Fails

This approach works because it aligns with how humans actually function. We have a deep psychological need to remain consistent with our self-image. When you try to act against your identity, you're fighting an uphill battle. When you align your actions with your identity, you're working with your psychology instead of against it.

Most people fail at change because they skip the identity work. They try to achieve different results while remaining the same person. But lasting change happens when you become a different person.

The One Question That Changes Everything

Whenever you're facing a decision, ask yourself: "What would someone with my desired identity do in this situation?"

This single question has guided me through countless decisions and consistently pointed me toward actions that align with who I want to become.

Remember: You don't need to be perfect. You just need to be consistent enough to prove to yourself that you are becoming the person you want to be.

This lesson took me years to learn because I kept trying to change from the outside in. But change happens from the inside out. Start with your identity, and everything else will follow.

The person you are today is the result of thousands of small votes you've cast for your identity. The person you'll be in five years will be the result of the votes you start casting today.

Choose wisely. Your future self is counting on it.
